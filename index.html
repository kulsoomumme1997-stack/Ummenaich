Context

A gap in a €12B company's ecosystem

Essity — the company behind TENA, Tork, and Tempo — had a physical product portfolio used by millions of people managing a persistent, stigmatised health condition — one most people dealt with in silence, without adequate support, and without anyone talking openly about it.

There was no digital layer. No way to break the taboo, grow market share, or build the recurring relationship that unlocks new revenue streams. The opportunity: build a digital health companion that could do all of it — deepen engagement with existing customers, bring new ones in, and give users something that actually helped.

The constraint: build it inside a regulated, 46,000-person FMCG company — navigating GDPR, medical device requirements, tight budgets, and compressed timelines — at startup speed, without a playbook, and without knowing yet which product or format would stick.

Case Study 02 · Teen Wellness · First Venture

Routeen — Gamified Wellness App for Teens

Context

Reaching customers before brand loyalty exists

Teens navigating puberty (12–17) feel insecure and alone on the journey. Their only sources: mothers, friends, Google, social media. No dedicated, trustworthy solution existed — and mothers were willing to help and pay, but didn't know how.

For Essity, this was a Point of Market Entry play: connect with consumers at the earliest possible life stage — before brand loyalties form — with the highest total customer lifetime value of any audience. Routeen was the bet: a gamified wellness companion designed to feel like the social apps teens already live in, not a health tool their parents picked.

This was my first venture at Essity — where I learned the ways of working I'd later apply end-to-end on Sammy.

The product

Built to feel like the apps teens already love

A live product — shipped on the App Store and Google Play. Personalised routines, gamified challenges, self-care games, and a reward system — expert-backed underneath, but designed to feel like the social apps teens use daily, not a health tool their parents picked.

Discovery

Built with a committed teen community

The original product thesis assumed a single engagement mechanic — a daily routine — would be enough to retain users. 100+ interviews with teens and parents, face-to-face group sessions, and 300+ surveys proved it wrong.

The real finding: teens needed multiple layered mechanics to keep coming back. Routines alone weren't enough. That insight drove entirely new feature categories that weren't in the original roadmap — points, marketplace, challenges — each shipped and validated with the community that helped design them.

The Pivot

We tested three business hypotheses. One failed. That was the point.

Routeen wasn't just testing features — it was testing how the venture could make money. Three business models, each with a clear validation threshold:

Self-monetising subscription
✗ Not validated
0% paying users — teens engaged, but wouldn't pay.
Lead generation for eCommerce
✓ Validated
18% reward point redemption — real purchase intent.
Market entry driver for femcare
✓ Validated
38% engagement with femcare content — the audience was listening.

The conclusion wrote itself: Routeen's value wasn't as a standalone subscription product — it was as a digital gateway, bringing the next generation of customers into the ecosystem before any competitor reached them. That evidence shaped the final recommendation to leadership. Not every venture's destiny is to scale alone; some prove their value by showing the organisation a new way to grow.

Context

A venture unit no one knows about is a venture unit that can't operate

Corporate venture units live and die by visibility. Internally, without it you lose resourcing, executive backing, and cross-functional cooperation. Externally, without it the best startups don't apply and the right partners don't engage. Essity Ventures was a small team inside a 46,000-person organisation — and I owned the work that made it visible on both sides.

This wasn't a comms role. It was running the full operational and reputational engine of the venture unit — continuous internal communications, an annual external startup event, and a scouting pipeline I migrated from an external supplier to an internal system end-to-end.
